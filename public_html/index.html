Growth Cycle - The 4 Stages

1. Germination stage:
This involves the initial sprouting of the seed. The seed is soaked in water or planted directly into soil until it sprouts.

2. Vegetative stage:
During this stage, the plant develops its leaves, stems, and roots. This stage can last anywhere from a few weeks to several months, depending on the strain and growing conditions.

3. Flowering stage:
In this stage, the plant begins to produce buds and flowers. This stage typically lasts around 8-10 weeks and is when the plant begins to produce the psychoactive compounds that are associated with cannabis use.

4. Harvesting and drying:
Once the plant has matured, it is harvested and dried before it can be consumed or processed further. The timing of harvesting can vary depending on the desired effect and the strain being grown. key concepts

Cannabis Anatomy

The mature cannabis plant consists of several key components, each serving a distinct purpose.

Here is a short summary of these components:

Flowers/Buds/Cola:
The flowers or buds of the cannabis plant contain the highest concentrations of cannabinoids, the most well known being

THC(tetrahydrocannabinol) and CBD(cannabidiol)

These compounds are responsible for the plant's psychoactive and therapeutic effects and there are actually nearly 100 compounds with medicinal use value in smaller proportion in commonly available cannabis.

Leaves:
Cannabis leaves are typically characterized by their jagged,finger-like leaflets. While they contain lower concentrations of cannabinoids compared to the flowers, they still possess some medicinal value. Leaves are often used to make cannabis-infused products like topical salves and tinctures.

Trichomes
Trichomes are tiny, mushroom-shaped structures found on the surface of cannabis flowers, leaves, and stems. They produce and store the cannabinoids, terpenes, and other compounds that give the plant its unique aroma, flavor, and therapeutic properties.

Stems
Cannabis stems are the sturdy, fibrous structures that support the plant's overall framework. They are generally less potent in terms of cannabinoids and are often discarded or used for purposes like making fiber or extracting lesser-known cannabinoids.x

Seeds:
Cannabis seeds, when mature, have a hard outer shell and can be used for propagation to grow new plants. While seeds do not contain significant levels of cannabinoids, they are valuable for breeding purposes and as a source of nutritious hemp seeds.

Roots: Cannabis roots play a crucial role in absorbing water and nutrients from the soil, providing stability to the plant. They also serve as a storage site for certain compounds. Although not commonly used, cannabis roots can be brewed to make teas or herbal preparations.

These components collectively contribute to the diverse range of applications and uses of the cannabis plant, including recreational and medicinal purposes, fiber production, and more.

It should be remembered that cannabis follows human beings all across the earth and is now found on every continent. Humans have used cannabis for both material and medicinal utility and its modern varieties are completely a product of its long-term cultivation.

Hygiene

Health benefits of controlling your own garden.
When you grow your own garden you are in complete control of your fertilizer and pesticide usage.

Pesticides
they come in natural and synthetic versions.
The biggest difference between the two is the method of action. Many organic methods will capture pests to incapacitate and hopefully suffocate them.

Synthetic pesticides
tend to be concentrated insect harming poisons. While there are organic poisons available as well its best to remember that poisons should always be used as a last resort to save a harvest as cleaning a plant off of those sprayed on poisons by washing the plant down with water can cultivate mold.

Mold/Mildew
this is one of the hardest hurdles growers come across. The fungus most commonly afflicting cannabis are Powdery Mildew in the 3rd stage of growth which is characterized by white furry layers first showing up underneath cannabis fan leaves and later covering the majority of fan leaves if left untended.

Another common fungus Botrytis commonly known as "Bud Rot" in the 4th stage of growth which is characterized by black mold forming within the flower

If we look closer there are hundreds of types of mold and mildew that love cannabis plants but all of them will show similar characteristics on the plants should you come across them.

Timely watering/fertilization dry plants can develop diseases and mutations in order to adapt to a dry environment. This can result in smaller plants, smaller flowers, and weaker trichrome production.

Cultivation

Tending a cannabis garden can be as easy or as difficult as you have the desire to make it. To make the best choices its good to know some basics about the cannabis species and how cannabis is categorized.

Phenotype Cannabis is the genus of the plant commonly used for its psychoactive effects in modern times. Cannabis is often categorized as "Sativa" or "Indica". This perceived difference comes from some characteristics of the psychoactive effects.

In order to clear up some confusion around the two "types" of cannabis, it is important to know that many modern cultivars are bred with plants from around the world. The genetic growth characteristics could come from a multitude of parents far back in a plants lineage.

This means that "Sativa" plants are often associated with tall and over long blooming periods. However, the distinction is not so clear between the two types.

"Indica" plants are believed to grow short and stout and perform well in cold weather This is an over-simplification as some tall plants do well in overly cold environments while some short ones do not.

What is important to remember about this is that the types allow for a categorization of psychoactive effects amongst users providing a common language about the products of cannabis cultivation.

"Indica" effects are considered to be relaxing and slow a user down providing anxiety relief.
"Sativa" effects are considered to be more euphoric, energetic, and anxiety inducing.
"Hybrid" effects are the result of the aforementioned mash of lineages.

The important fact I have come to learn is that each plant will respond according to the genetics its parents provide it with. its prior lineage and phenotype will determine the possible outcomes and only having grown a plant to fruition would a grower truly know how a specific "phenotype" grows and what quality of flower it provides.

This means that determining outcomes is not as cut and dry as "Sativa" and "Indica" as the genetic origins of the plant you obtain may well have physical and psychoactive characteristics of either.

Often with the help of websites such as SeedFinder we can look back through the known data of lineages and determine some characteristics of plants we come across.

Tips and Tricks!

to get started Proper planning will save you time and headache down the road. Decisions like
"How many plants?",
"What kind of garden will I have? Indoor or Outdoor?",
"What plants am I interested in?"

It is always a good idea to determine the amount of time you wish to put into a garden as Cannabis is a thirsty plant and to get usable medicine in the end requires dedication and an eye for detail.
Rules of thumb that I live by when considering starting a new cultivation are as follows.
- Know the weather and conditions! you can get tools to measure the humidity of the space (especially for indoor gardens), temperature, and Ph of the water you are using.
Temperature is key The ideal temperature for cannabis varies slightly over its growth stages. If you grow outside then consider your weather forecasts to determine when to start your cultivation. If you are growing indoors then in stage 1 the temperature should be higher than 76 degrees but not higher than 90 degrees f. By stage 2 you should be finding a balance in between 75 degrees and 85 degrees. Stage 3 will call for lower temperatures close to 76 degrees. stage 4 will see you wanting to cool them down even more to about 70 degrees.
- A proper Ph for cannabis feeding is between 5.8 and 6.4. You can find amendments to water Ph at your local gardening store.
- Coco is easy! As a growing medium it is both fairly inexpensive and makes it easy for the cultivars to uptake the available elements in your fertilizer. You can find coco in most gardening stores and I recommend it for beginners.
- I recommend setting up your plants either with stakes to hold up heavy branches or a grid trellis system to pull them through and support the plants stems as they buds get heavier. this will also allow the plant to devote more of its limited energies to growing flowers and not stem.
